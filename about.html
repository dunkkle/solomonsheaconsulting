About me

I've been in tech since I was a kid. I grew up in Santa Barbara building PCs with my dad and my brothers, wiring up his office network, and messing around with websites way back in the day. That carried through to UCLA, where I was building little programs and file-sharing networks in my dorm room and working at the Student Technology Center, helping students get online.

After college I went into web development as a freelancer and consultant. I built websites for restaurants and local small businesses, mostly the practical stuff: menus, scheduling, taking reservations online. I also did a stint with a startup in the private jet industry, building flight scheduling software.

My first corporate job was AppFolio, and I stayed for eight years. I came in as a web developer and pretty quickly started leading a team. That's where I moved into what software companies call post-sales: the part of the business responsible for actually delivering the product to customers. By the end I was managing a team of 27 designers, engineers, consultants, and product managers, with P&L responsibility for about $15 million. We did a lot there that I'm still proud of. We migrated 5,500 customer websites to a new platform without any downtime. We built our own DNS management app from scratch. We cut customer onboarding from 120 days to under 30.

After AppFolio I held leadership roles at software companies in real estate and insurance, and most recently built the client success function at an AI consulting firm. Different companies, same territory: the messy, human part of software, where a product either becomes part of how a team works or quietly gets abandoned.

Somewhere in there, my wife and I built an e-commerce business together. She had a successful business creating paper flower art for retail displays and weddings, and over time we turned that service into a product: biodegradable urns for burials at sea, made from her paper flowers. It's an unusual niche (her work has been featured on Martha Stewart's show), and running it together taught me what every small-business owner knows about wearing all the hats at once.

These days I live in San Clemente with my wife and our two kids. I grew up racing sailboats, including the 2011 Transpac from LA to Honolulu, but the kids keep me busy enough that golf has taken over what free time is left. My DJ gear is packed away, though it's still there in case somebody calls me out of retirement.

Solomon Shea is the work I've always liked best, done on my own shingle. Figuring out what technology can actually do for a business, building it, and sticking around until the team runs with it. Solomon is my wife's family name; Shea is mine.
